The Accidental Revolution

GPUs were built for video games — rendering millions of pixels. Each pixel is one tiny math problem. AI training is also millions of tiny math problems. The architectures aligned by accident.

AI Is Getting Accessible

AI models are shrinking to fit on everyday hardware.

AI models are measured in parameters — the millions or billions of learned values that make them work. More parameters generally means smarter AI, but also means more expensive hardware to run it.

GPT-3 (2020)
175 billion parameters
Llama 3 (2024)
8 billion parameters

GPT-3 (the model behind early ChatGPT) needed an entire data center. Llama 3 — Meta's open-source AI model, released for free — can run on a regular gaming computer. That's a 20x reduction in size.

This is possible thanks to quantization — a technique that compresses AI models to use less memory (like converting a high-res photo to a smaller file without losing much quality). Researchers like Georgi Gerganov, who created a tool called llama.cpp, made it possible for anyone with a decent computer to run AI locally — no cloud subscription needed.

Andrej Karpathy, former head of AI at Tesla and now an AI educator on YouTube, has helped millions of people understand how these systems work. The barrier to entry — both in knowledge and hardware — is falling fast.

The People Behind the Hardware

The AI hardware revolution didn't happen by accident — these are the people whose decisions, inventions, and companies shaped the technology.

GH

Geoffrey Hinton

Known as the "Godfather of AI." Won the Nobel Prize for his work on neural networks — the math that made GPUs essential for AI. Left Google in 2023 to warn about AI risks.

JH

Jensen Huang

CEO of NVIDIA, the company that makes ~86% of AI chips. His decision to release CUDA in 2007 turned gaming GPUs into AI hardware. NVIDIA is now worth over $3 trillion.

LS

Lisa Su

CEO of AMD — NVIDIA's biggest competitor. Under her leadership, AMD became the only real alternative for AI chips, forcing prices down and pushing innovation.

GM

Gordon Moore

Co-founder of Intel. Created "Moore's Law" in 1965 — the observation that the number of transistors on a chip doubles every two years. This prediction guided chip design for 60 years.

FL

Fei-Fei Li

Stanford professor who created ImageNet — the massive image database that proved GPUs could beat traditional AI. The 2012 AlexNet moment that started the GPU revolution used her dataset.

SA

Sam Altman

CEO of OpenAI, the company behind ChatGPT. ChatGPT's explosion in 2022 created massive demand for AI chips, turning GPU supply into a geopolitical issue. Lobbied for $7 trillion in chip investment.

AK

Andrej Karpathy

Former head of AI at Tesla (self-driving cars). Now an AI educator on YouTube with millions of viewers. Built the neural network that powers Tesla's Autopilot — running on custom AI chips in every Tesla.

DA

Dario Amodei

CEO of Anthropic (maker of Claude AI). Left OpenAI to focus on AI safety — making sure AI systems are reliable and safe. Anthropic's research pushes for responsible AI development.

JK

Jim Keller

Legendary chip architect who designed processors at AMD, Apple, Tesla, and Intel. The silicon in your iPhone, your PlayStation, and Tesla's self-driving computer — he designed the chips behind all of them.

What's Next

🔬 Photonic Computing

Using light instead of electricity. Near-zero latency, massive bandwidth.

🧱 3D Stacked Chips

Building upward, not outward. More transistors in less space.

🧠 Neuromorphic Chips

Hardware that mimics biological neurons. Intel's Loihi, IBM's TrueNorth.

🧩 Chiplets

Modular chip design. Mix specialized components like building blocks.

The shift from "one chip does everything" to "specialized silicon for specific tasks" is a fundamental change in computing.
