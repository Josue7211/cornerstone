**Part 1: Research Narrative**

**Section 1: Scope (approx. 200-250 words)**
Heading: `## Scope`

Define the research project and its parameters:
- **Research topic:** The history of AI hardware architecture — how GPUs evolved from graphics rendering to AI acceleration, the emergence of specialized chips (TPUs, NPUs), and what this means for the future of computing and equitable access to AI.
- **Research questions (state all 3):**
  1. How has GPU architecture evolved from a graphics rendering device to an AI accelerator, and what specific design features facilitated this evolution?
  2. What is the fundamental architectural difference between general-purpose processors (CPUs) and AI-specialized hardware (GPUs, TPUs, NPUs)?
  3. What does AI-specialized hardware mean for the future of computing and future access to AI?
- **Genre/medium:** The final project is an interactive scroll-driven website built with GSAP and ScrollTrigger — a visual, animated experience that walks the audience through the hardware evolution narrative. NOT a PowerPoint or essay. The medium was chosen because the subject (visual hardware architecture) demands visual presentation, and building the website with AI tools creates a meta-narrative (using AI to explain AI hardware).
- **Background research:** Used FSW Libraries (Credo Reference for foundational definitions), ACM Digital Library and Google Scholar for peer-reviewed papers, and authoritative industry sources for current data. The research expanded into a full 13,000+ word paper with 35 sources — far beyond the course requirement — because the topic demanded comprehensive treatment.

Cite: (Glassner, 2012; Jouppi et al., 2017; Data Center Knowledge, 2025)

**Section 2: Approach/Perspective (approx. 200-250 words)**
Heading: `## Approach and Perspective`

Define critical lens, methods, philosophy:
- **Critical lens:** Democratization of technology — examining who has access to AI hardware and how that access is shifting from centralized cloud infrastructure to consumer devices. This lens emerged from personal experience: running AI models locally on consumer hardware that would have required a data center three years ago.
- **Methods/Modes of analysis:**
  1. Change over time — tracing NVIDIA GPU architecture across 7 generations (Fermi 2010 → Blackwell 2024)
  2. Compare and contrast — CPU vs GPU vs TPU vs NPU architectural differences
  3. Cause and effect — how AI model complexity drove hardware specialization
- **What college courses shaped this:** Computer courses gave the technical foundation for understanding processor architectures. IDS2891 provided the interdisciplinary framework — connecting engineering to economics (who can afford AI hardware), ethics (autonomous weapons, deepfakes), and social equity (2.6 billion people without internet can't access cloud AI).
- **Why this matters:** AI is reshaping every industry. The hardware layer determines who can participate. Understanding it is becoming civic literacy, not just engineering knowledge.
- **Background knowledge:** Built a desktop PC with an NVIDIA RTX 4070 Ti SUPER, run a homelab with local AI models through Ollama. This firsthand experience with local AI inference informed the research angle — the democratization thesis is not theoretical but personal.

Cite: (CRV Science, 2024; Rathore, 2024; IBM, 2024)

**Section 3: Process and Plan (approx. 200-250 words)**
Heading: `## Process and Plan`

Describe the research process and execution plan:
- **How research started:** Library assignments (Credo Reference) provided foundational definitions — GPU (Glassner, 2012), parallel processing (Daintith & Wright, 2008), AI and the fourth industrial revolution (Marcovitz, 2018). These tertiary sources established vocabulary and scope.
- **How it deepened:** Moved to peer-reviewed literature — Jouppi et al. (2017) on Google's TPU was the most important single source, providing the technical foundation for understanding why specialized AI chips exist. Sanmartín & Prohaska (2023) offered a survey of TPU applications.
- **How scope expanded:** Initial research was purely technical (how GPUs work). Through the process, the scope expanded to include: software optimization (model compression, token optimization), open-source AI ecosystems, psychology of cloud dependence, geopolitics (US-China chip export controls), and economic implications (UBI). The research paper grew to 13,000+ words because these threads were interconnected.
- **Execution plan for final project:** The interactive website will present the hardware evolution narrative visually — using animated architecture diagrams, scroll-driven transitions, and layered information that lets the audience explore at their own pace. The presentation covers the core narrative; the website provides depth. At least 5 sources will be integrated into the final artifact.

Cite: (Daintith & Wright, 2008; Sanmartín & Prohaska, 2023; Bitdeer, 2025)

**Section 4: Context (approx. 200-250 words)**
Heading: `## Context`

Define audience, scholarly conversation, GE connections:
- **Audience:** Computer engineering students, tech enthusiasts, and anyone curious about how hardware enables modern AI. Accessible to non-technical readers without dumbing down the nuances. The interactive website format lowers the barrier — visual architecture diagrams communicate what text alone cannot.
- **Scholarly conversation:** This research sits at the intersection of computer architecture, AI/ML research, and science & technology studies (STS). The key scholarly tension: whether AI should remain centralized (Altman's safety argument) or be democratized (open-source advocates). Sources like Jouppi et al. (2017), Krizhevsky et al. (2012), and industry analyses from Data Center Knowledge (2025) represent the technical side; the democratization argument draws on open-source ecosystem analysis and economic projections.
- **GE course connections:** This project connects interdisciplinary thinking from across general education — economics (market concentration, UBI), ethics (autonomous weapons, privacy), political science (chip export controls as geopolitical weapons), psychology (why people trust cloud over local), and communication (presenting technical content to non-technical audiences). IDS2891 taught me to recognize these connections as features, not tangents.
- **Background reflection:** The most surprising discovery was how psychological the barriers to local AI are — not technical. The "delegation premium" concept and the legitimacy illusion (free = inferior) explained adoption patterns that pure technical analysis could not.

Cite: (Krizhevsky et al., 2012; UST, 2024; Meta AI, 2024)
