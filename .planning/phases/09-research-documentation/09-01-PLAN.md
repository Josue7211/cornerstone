**Section 1: How I Found My Topic (approx. 150-180 words)**
Heading: `## How I Found My Topic`

Narrative: I built my own desktop PC from scratch and run a homelab infrastructure at home. The GPU in that machine — an NVIDIA RTX 4070 Ti SUPER — is architecturally related to the hardware training AI models in data centers. That personal connection made me ask a question I had never seen answered in plain language: how did a chip designed for video games become the engine of the AI revolution? My initial interest was broad — computer hardware in general — but through the library research process it narrowed decisively. I discovered that the democratization angle was the most under-examined thread: not just what GPUs do, but who gets to use them and what happens as that access expands beyond data centers.

Cite here: (Glassner, 2012) for GPU definition; (Marcovitz, 2018) for AI/ML context; (Data Center Knowledge, 2025) for market concentration framing.

**Section 3: How My Thinking Evolved (approx. 150-180 words)**
Heading: `## How My Thinking Evolved`

Narrative: My research questions emerged from the background research, not before it. The original framing was purely technical — "how does a GPU work?" By the time I had read the foundational sources, the question had sharpened into something more interesting: why did GPU architecture, specifically, become dominant for AI when Google's TPU is technically more efficient for inference (Jouppi et al., 2017)? That question led to the democratization thread — GPUs won because they were already everywhere, developers already knew them through CUDA, and the ecosystem had 15 years of momentum (CRV Science, 2024). The third research question — about equitable access — emerged last, prompted by NVIDIA's 86% market share figure (Data Center Knowledge, 2025) and the UST market growth projections (UST, 2024). I realized the hardware story is also a power concentration story.

Explicitly state the three research questions here:
1. How has GPU architecture evolved from a graphics rendering device to an AI accelerator, and what specific design features facilitated this evolution? (Mode: change over time)
2. What is the fundamental architectural difference between CPUs, GPUs, TPUs, and NPUs? (Mode: compare and contrast)
3. What does the development of AI-specialized hardware mean for the future of computing and equitable access to AI? (Mode: cause and effect)

Cite here: (IBM, 2024); (Introl, 2024).

**Section 5: Key Findings and Implications (approx. 150-180 words)**
Heading: `## Key Findings and What They Mean`

Narrative: Three findings stood out. First, CUDA (2007) was the real inflection point — not the GPU itself but its programmability (CRV Science, 2024). Second, the AI chip market is projected to grow from $29.65 billion in 2024 to $164 billion by 2029 (UST, 2024), a scale that will reshape the semiconductor industry. Third, the architectural diversity emerging — GPUs, TPUs, NPUs, custom ASICs — suggests the "general-purpose" era of computing may be ending. For a non-technical audience, the implication is straightforward: the hardware layer determines who can participate in AI. Right now, that layer is highly concentrated. As local AI hardware (consumer GPUs, NPU-equipped laptops, edge devices) becomes more capable, the democratization potential grows — but so does the risk of a two-tier AI ecosystem where cloud-dependent users and local-compute users have fundamentally different capabilities. This is why hardware literacy matters beyond engineering.

Cite here: (IBM, 2024); (Built In, 2024); (Data Center Knowledge, 2025).
