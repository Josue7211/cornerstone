## 1. Identify Your Audience

My research focuses on AI hardware architecture — specifically, how GPUs evolved from graphics rendering devices into the backbone of modern artificial intelligence, and how specialized chips like TPUs and NPUs are pushing computing into a new era. My primary audience is college students and young adults (18–30) who use AI-powered tools daily — whether that's ChatGPT, image generators, voice assistants, or recommendation algorithms — but have no idea what hardware makes it all work. Most people in this demographic interact with AI constantly without ever thinking about the silicon underneath.

My secondary audience includes tech enthusiasts who may understand software but haven't explored the hardware side, as well as anyone curious about where computing is headed. This group would care about my research because the hardware powering AI directly affects what AI can do, how much it costs, and who has access to it. Understanding this is the difference between being a consumer of technology and being someone who actually comprehends what's driving the most transformative technology of our generation.

## 2. Analyze Audience Needs and Preferences

My audience is predominantly visual and interactive learners who are accustomed to consuming information through websites, videos, and social media rather than printed text. They respond best to content that is visually engaging, broken into digestible segments, and uses analogies or comparisons rather than raw technical jargon. A wall of text about systolic arrays and FLOPS would lose them in seconds.

The biggest barriers are: (1) the assumption that hardware topics are "too technical" or boring, (2) unfamiliarity with terms like GPU, TPU, NPU, or CUDA, and (3) the abstract nature of chip architecture — you can't see data flowing through silicon the way you can see a website loading. My project needs to bridge that gap by making the invisible visible through visual schematics, animations, and side-by-side comparisons that show *how* these processors actually differ.

## 3. Propose Creative Presentation Ideas

**Modality A: Interactive Website with Visual Architecture Schematics**

I want to build an interactive, scroll-driven website that walks visitors through the evolution of AI hardware. Each section would visually compare how data moves through a CPU versus a GPU versus a TPU, using animated diagrams and simplified schematics. Users could click through a timeline from early CPUs to NVIDIA's first CUDA-enabled GPUs to Google's TPU to Apple's Neural Engine. The website itself would be built using AI-assisted development tools, which adds a meta layer — the project about AI hardware was created *by* AI, running on the very hardware being discussed. This format is ideal because my audience lives on the web, it allows self-paced exploration, and it demonstrates technical capability while communicating research findings. No essay or slideshow can achieve this level of engagement.

**Modality B: Short Video Documentary with Animated Chip Comparisons**

As a supporting element, I would create a short (5–8 minute) video documentary that explains the key concepts from my research using motion graphics. Animated sequences would show how parallel processing works visually — imagine 1,000 small cores processing data simultaneously compared to 8 large cores handling tasks one at a time. The video format is effective because it combines narration with visual demonstration, making abstract architectural concepts tangible. It can be shared easily and rewatched, which is important for a topic where the concepts build on each other.
